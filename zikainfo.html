Zika Virus: An Overview

General

The Zika virus is a pathogen transmitted by the Aedes species mosquito. Belonging to the Flavivirus genus, Flaviviridae family, and Spondweni group, the virus was first found in a monkey in 1947 in the Zika forest, Uganda, and in 1952 in a human in Nigeria ("The history of Zika Virus").

Prevention

There is no preventive treatment or vaccine for the Zika virus, but certain precautions can be taken to reduce the chance of infection, including

  • Wearing long-sleeved shirts or long pants
  • Using insect/mosquito repellan
  • Staying indoors with air conditioning or screen door
  • Sleeping under mosquito nets, which may be especially helpful for children under 2 months of age
  • Abstinence or the use of condoms to prevent the sexual transmission of Zika

Diagnosis

While symptoms of Zika are often similar to those of other mosquito-borne diseases, such as dengue and chikungunya, a blood or urine test can be used to confirm the presence of the virus.

Methods of transmission

  • Mosquito bites
  • Pregnant mother to child
  • Sexual transmission through intercourse
  • Blood transfusions or organ transplantation

Symptoms

Mild symptoms last for about 2 - 7 days, with rare hospitalization. No fatalities have yet to be recorded. Symptoms include rash, mild fever, arthralgia (joint pain), fatigue and headache. The rash often begins in the face and spreads to the rest of the body. Among pregnant women, Zika may cause damage to a fetus's central nervous system, sometimes resulting in microcephaly or Guillain-Barré syndrome, among others ("Zika Virus"). - Vassilios Kaxiras
